<p>A <b>video game developer</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Software_developer" title="Software developer">software developer</a> that specializes in <a href="/wiki/Video_game_development" title="Video game development">video game development</a> – the process and related disciplines of creating <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> A game developer can range from one person who undertakes all tasks<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> to a large business with employee responsibilities split between individual disciplines, such as <a href="/wiki/Game_programming" title="Game programming">programming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Video_game_design" title="Video game design">design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Game_art_design" title="Game art design">art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Game_testing" title="Game testing">testing</a>, etc. Most game development companies have <a href="/wiki/Video_game_publisher" title="Video game publisher">video game publisher</a> financial and usually marketing support.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Self-funded developers are known as independent or indie developers and usually make <a href="/wiki/Indie_games" class="mw-redirect" title="Indie games">indie games</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IGMWhatIsIndie_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGMWhatIsIndie-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A developer may specialize in a certain <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">video game console</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Switch" title="Nintendo Switch">Nintendo Switch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Xbox_One" title="Xbox One">Xbox One</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a>'s <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_4" title="PlayStation 4">PlayStation 4</a>), or may develop for a number of systems (including <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mobile_devices" class="mw-redirect" title="Mobile devices">mobile devices</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Video-game developers specialize in certain types of games (such as <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video games</a> or <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooters</a>). Some focus on <a href="/wiki/Porting" title="Porting">porting</a> games from one system to another, or translating games from one language to another. Less commonly, some do software-development work in addition to games.
</p><p>Most <a href="/wiki/Video_game_publisher" title="Video game publisher">video game publishers</a> maintain development studios (such as <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a>'s <a href="/wiki/EA_Canada" class="mw-redirect" title="EA Canada">EA Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Square_Enix" title="Square Enix">Square Enix</a>'s studios, <a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Radical_Entertainment" title="Radical Entertainment">Radical Entertainment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_EAD" class="mw-redirect" title="Nintendo EAD">Nintendo EAD</a> and Sony's <a href="/wiki/Polyphony_Digital" title="Polyphony Digital">Polyphony Digital</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naughty_Dog" title="Naughty Dog">Naughty Dog</a>). However, since publishing is still their primary activity they are generally described as "publishers" rather than "developers". Developers may be private as well (such as how <a href="/wiki/Bungie" title="Bungie">Bungie</a> was, the company which developed the <a href="/wiki/Halo_(series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Halo (series)"><i>Halo</i> series</a> exclusive to Microsoft's <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Video_game_developer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First-party_developer">First-party developer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Video_game_developer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First-party developer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Video_game_industry" title="Video game industry">video game industry</a>, a first-party developer is part of a company which manufactures a <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">video game console</a> and develops exclusively for it. First-party developers may use the name of the company itself (such as <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a>), have a specific division name (such as <a href="/wiki/Sony_Computer_Entertainment" class="mw-redirect" title="Sony Computer Entertainment">Sony's</a> <a href="/wiki/Polyphony_Digital" title="Polyphony Digital">Polyphony Digital</a>) or have been an independent studio before being acquired by the console manufacturer (such as <a href="/wiki/Rare_(company)" title="Rare (company)">Rare</a> or <a href="/wiki/Naughty_Dog" title="Naughty Dog">Naughty Dog</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Whether by purchasing an independent studio or by founding a new team, the acquisition of a first-party developer involves a huge <a href="/wiki/Financial_investment" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial investment">financial investment</a> on the part of the console manufacturer, which is wasted if the developer fails to produce a hit game in a timely manner.<sup id="cite_ref-NGen30_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NGen30-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, using first-party developers saves the cost of having to make royalty payments on a game's profits.<sup id="cite_ref-NGen30_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NGen30-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second-party_developer">Second-party developer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Video_game_developer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Second-party developer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Second-party developer is a colloquial term often used by gaming enthusiasts and media to describe game studios who take development contracts from platform holders and produce games exclusive to that platform.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> As a balance to not being able to release their game for other platforms, second-party developers are usually offered higher royalty rates than third-party developers.<sup id="cite_ref-NGen30_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NGen30-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> These studios may have exclusive publishing agreements (or other business relationships) with the platform holder, but maintain independence so upon completion or termination of their contracts are able to continue developing games. Examples are <a href="/wiki/Insomniac_Games" title="Insomniac Games">Insomniac Games</a> (originally a 2nd party for Sony), <a href="/wiki/Bungie" title="Bungie">Bungie</a> (originally a 2nd party for Microsoft) and <a href="/wiki/Rareware" class="mw-redirect" title="Rareware">Rareware</a> (originally a 2nd party for Nintendo).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third-party_developers">Third-party developers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Video_game_developer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Third-party developers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span id="Third-party_developers"></span><span id="3rd-party_developers"></span>
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Third-party_software_component" title="Third-party software component">Third-party software component</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision</a> in 1979 became the first third-party video game developer,<sup id="cite_ref-mj19821226_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mj19821226-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> where the term "second-party" originally referred to the consumers. A third-party developer may also publish games, or work for a video game publisher to develop a title. Both publisher and developer have considerable input in the game's <a href="/wiki/Game_design" title="Game design">design</a> and content. However, the publisher's wishes generally override those of the developer.
</p><p>The business arrangement between the developer and publisher is governed by a <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a>, which specifies a list of <a href="/wiki/Milestone_(project_management)" title="Milestone (project management)">milestones</a> intended to be delivered over a period of time. By updating its milestones, the publisher verifies that work is progressing quickly enough to meet its deadline and can direct the developer if the game is not meeting expectations. When each milestone is completed (and accepted), the publisher pays the developer an advance on <a href="/wiki/Royalties" class="mw-redirect" title="Royalties">royalties</a>. Successful developers may maintain several teams working on different games for different publishers. Generally, however, third-party developers tend to be small, close-knit teams. Third-party game development is a volatile sector, since small developers may be dependent on income from a single publisher; one canceled game may be devastating to a small developer. Because of this, many small development companies are short-lived.
</p><p>A common <a href="/wiki/Exit_strategy" title="Exit strategy">exit strategy</a> for a successful video-game developer is to sell the company to a publisher, becoming an in-house developer. In-house development teams tend to have more freedom in the design and content of a game compared to third-party developers. One reason is that since the developers are employees of the publisher, their interests are aligned with those of the publisher; the publisher may spend less effort ensuring that the developer's decisions do not enrich the developer at the publisher's expense.
</p><p>In recent years, larger publishers have acquired several third-party developers. While these development teams are now technically "in-house", they often continue to operate in an autonomous manner (with their own culture and work practices). For example, Activision acquired <a href="/wiki/Raven_Software" title="Raven Software">Raven</a> (1997); <a href="/wiki/Neversoft" title="Neversoft">Neversoft</a> (1999), which merged with <a href="/wiki/Infinity_Ward" title="Infinity Ward">Infinity Ward</a> in 2014; <a href="/wiki/Z-Axis" class="mw-redirect" title="Z-Axis">Z-Axis</a> (2001); <a href="/wiki/Treyarch" title="Treyarch">Treyarch</a> (2001); <a href="/wiki/Luxoflux" title="Luxoflux">Luxoflux</a> (2002); <a href="/wiki/Shaba_Games" title="Shaba Games">Shaba</a> (2002); <a href="/wiki/Infinity_Ward" title="Infinity Ward">Infinity Ward</a> (2003) and <a href="/wiki/Vicarious_Visions" title="Vicarious Visions">Vicarious Visions</a> (2005). All these developers continue operating much as they did before acquisition, the primary differences being exclusivity and financial details. Publishers tend to be more forgiving of their own development teams going over budget (or missing deadlines) than third-party developers.
</p><p>A developer may not be the primary entity creating a piece of software, usually providing an external software tool which helps organize (or use) information for the primary software product. Such tools may be a <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">database</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voice_over_IP" title="Voice over IP">Voice over IP</a>, or add-in <a href="/wiki/Interface_(computer_science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Interface (computer science)">interface</a> software; this is also known as <a href="/wiki/Middleware" title="Middleware">middleware</a>. A good example of this is <a href="/wiki/SpeedTree" title="SpeedTree">SpeedTree</a> developed by Interactive Data Visualization Inc.
</p><p>In addition, accessories like <a href="/wiki/Headset_(audio)" title="Headset (audio)">headsets</a> may be known as third-party headsets; the company manufacturing the headset may be different from the console company. For example, <a href="/wiki/Turtle_Beach_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Turtle Beach Systems">Turtle Beach</a> is a third-party headset manufacturer for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xbox_360" title="Xbox 360">Xbox 360</a>. Also now including the <a href="/wiki/Xbox_One" title="Xbox One">Xbox One</a> and <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_4" title="PlayStation 4">PlayStation 4</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indie_game_developers">Indie game developers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Video_game_developer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Indie game developers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Indie_game" title="Indie game">Indie game</a> and <a href="/wiki/Independent_video_game_development" title="Independent video game development">Independent video game development</a></div>
<p>Independents are software developers which are not owned by (or dependent on) a single publisher. Some of these developers self-publish their games, relying on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> and word of mouth for publicity. Without the large marketing budgets of mainstream publishers, their products may receive less recognition than those of larger publishers such as Sony, Microsoft or Nintendo. With the advent of digital distribution of inexpensive games on game consoles, it is now possible for indie game developers to forge agreements with console manufacturers for broad distribution of their games.
</p><p>Other indie game developers create game software for a number of video-game publishers on several gaming platforms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In recent years this model has been in decline; larger publishers, such as Electronic Arts and Activision, increasingly turn to internal studios (usually former independent developers acquired for their development needs).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quality_of_life">Quality of life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Video_game_developer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Quality of life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Video-game development is usually conducted in a casual business environment, with T-shirts and sandals common work attire. Many workers find this type of environment rewarding and pleasant professionally and personally.<sup id="cite_ref-manliness_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manliness-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, the industry also requires long working hours from its employees (sometimes to an extent seen as unsustainable).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Employee <a href="/wiki/Burnout_(psychology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Burnout (psychology)">burnout</a> is not uncommon.<sup id="cite_ref-manliness_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manliness-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An entry-level programmer can make, on average, over $66,000 annually only if they are successful in obtaining a position in a medium to large video game company.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> An experienced game-development employee, depending on his or her expertise and experience, averaged roughly $73,000 in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Indie game developers may only earn between $10,000 to $50,000 a year depending on how financially successful their titles are.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to being part of the software industry<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, game development is also within the <a href="/wiki/Entertainment" title="Entertainment">entertainment</a> industry; most sectors of the entertainment industry (such as <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">films</a> and <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>) require long working hours and dedication from their employees, such as willingness to relocate and/or required to develop games that do not appeal to their personal taste. The creative rewards of work in the entertainment business attracts <a href="/wiki/Workforce" title="Workforce">labor</a> to the industry, creating a competitive <a href="/wiki/Labor_market" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor market">labor market</a> which demands a high level of commitment and performance from employees. Industry communities, such as the <a href="/wiki/International_Game_Developers_Association" title="International Game Developers Association">International Game Developers Association</a> (IGDA), are conducting increasing discussions about the problem; they are concerned that working conditions in the industry cause significant deterioration in its employees' quality of life.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Crunch_time.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Crunch_time&quot;"><span id="Crunch_time"></span>"Crunch time"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Video_game_developer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: &quot;Crunch time&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some video game developers (such as <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a>) have been accused of the excessive invocation of "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/crunch_time" class="extiw" title="wikt:crunch time">crunch time</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> "Crunch time" is the point at which the team is thought to be failing to achieve milestones needed to launch a game on schedule. The complexity of work flow and the intangibles of artistic and aesthetic demands in video-game creation create difficulty in predicting milestones.
</p><p>Most game-development engineers and artists in the United States are considered <a href="/wiki/Salary" title="Salary">salaried employees</a>; as "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/exempt" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:exempt">exempt</a> non-hourly-paid professionals", they are not subject to state laws governing <a href="/wiki/Overtime" title="Overtime">overtime</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> An exception is <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, where software developers are specifically protected by a minimum hourly wage to be considered exempt.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In 2008, due to the amendment to California Labor Code Section 515.5 by Bill SB 929,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> the minimum wage was $36 per hour (or $74,880 per year).
</p><p>Attention to "crunching" was drawn by a 2004 <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blog</a> post entitled <i>ea_spouse</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The protest against crunch time was posted by <a href="/wiki/Erin_Hoffman" title="Erin Hoffman">Erin Hoffman</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fiancee" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiancee">fiancee</a> of Electronic Arts developer Leander Hasty), who contended that her life was being indirectly destroyed by the company's work policy. This led to debate in the industry but no visible changes until March 2005, when Electronic Arts announced internally that it was planning to extend overtime pay to some employees not currently eligible. Hasty and Hoffman later joined an independent development studio (<a href="/w/index.php?title=1st_Playable_Productions&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="1st Playable Productions (page does not exist)">1st Playable Productions</a>) and founded Gamewatch, a website for discussing the game-development environment industry-wide. As senior game developers age and family responsibilities become more important, many companies are moderating the worst crunch-time practices to attract better-quality staff.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A similar situation was brought to light in January 2010, when a collective group of "Rockstar Spouses", the spouses of developers at <a href="/wiki/Rockstar_San_Diego" title="Rockstar San Diego">Rockstar San Diego</a>, posted an open letter criticizing the management of the studio for deteriorating working conditions for their significant other since March 2009, which including excessive crunch time. This was followed by several former Rockstar employees posting similar complaints of their time there.<sup id="cite_ref-Eurogamer:_Rockstar_Spouse_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eurogamer:_Rockstar_Spouse-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GamesIndustry.biz:_Rockstar_Spouse_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GamesIndustry.biz:_Rockstar_Spouse-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Game_Developers_Association" title="International Game Developers Association">International Game Developers Association</a> considered that Rockstar's working conditions were exploitative and harmful.<sup id="cite_ref-Kotaku:_IGDA_Condemns_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kotaku:_IGDA_Condemns-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> A similar concern of crunch time at the same studio arose near the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Dead_Redemption_2" title="Red Dead Redemption 2">Red Dead Redemption 2</a></i> in October 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unionization">Unionization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Video_game_developer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Unionization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Similar to other tech industries, video game developers are typically not <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">unionized</a>. This is a result of the industry being driven more by creativity and innovation rather than production, the lack of distinction between management and employees in the white-collar area, and that the pace at which the industry moves that makes union actions difficult to plan out.<sup id="cite_ref-vice_union_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vice_union-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> However, when situations related to crunch time become prevalent in the news, there have typically been followup discussions towards the potential to form a union.<sup id="cite_ref-vice_union_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vice_union-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> A survey performed by the <a href="/wiki/International_Game_Developers_Association" title="International Game Developers Association">International Game Developers Association</a> in 2014 found that more than half of the 2,200 developers surveyed favored unionization.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, voice actors in the <a href="/wiki/SAG-AFTRA" title="SAG-AFTRA">Screen Actors Guild‐American Federation of Television and Radio Artists</a> (SAG-AFTRA) union doing work for video games struck several major publishers, demanding better <a href="/wiki/Royalty_payment" title="Royalty payment">royalty payments</a> and provisions related to the safety of their vocal performances, when their union's standard contract was up for renewal. The <a href="/wiki/2016%E2%80%9317_video_game_voice_actor_strike" title="2016–17 video game voice actor strike">voice actor strike</a> lasted for over 300 days into 2017 before a new deal was made between SAG-AFTRA and the publishers. While this had some effects on a few games within the industry, it brought to the forefront the question of whether video game developers should unionize.<sup id="cite_ref-vice_union_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vice_union-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A grassroots movement, Game Workers Unite, was established around 2017 to discuss and debate issues related to unionization of game developers. The group came to the forefront during the March 2018 <a href="/wiki/Game_Developers_Conference" title="Game Developers Conference">Game Developers Conference</a> by holding a roundtable discussion with the <a href="/wiki/International_Game_Developers_Association" title="International Game Developers Association">International Game Developers Association</a> (IDGA), the professional association for developers. Statements made by the IDGA's current executive director Jen MacLean relating to IGDA's activities had been seen by as anti-union, and Game Workers Unite desired to start a conversation to lay out the need for developers to unionize.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In the wake of the sudden near-closure of <a href="/wiki/Telltale_Games" title="Telltale Games">Telltale Games</a> in September 2018, the movement again called out for the industry to unionize. The movement argued that Telltale had not given any warning to its 250 employees let go, having hired additional staff as recently as a week prior, and left them without pensions or health-care options; it was further argued that the studio considered this a closure rather than layoffs, as to get around failure to notify required by the <a href="/wiki/Worker_Adjustment_and_Retraining_Notification_Act_of_1988" title="Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act of 1988">Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act of 1988</a> preceding layoffs.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The situation was argued to be "exploitive", as Telltale had been known to force its employees to frequently work under "crunch time" to deliver its games.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Women_in_game_development">Women in game development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Video_game_developer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Women in game development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Women_and_video_games#Women_in_the_games_industry" title="Women and video games">Women and video games §&#160;Women in the games industry</a></div>
<p>In 1989, according to <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i>, women constituted only 3% of the gaming industry.<sup id="cite_ref-variety2013_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-variety2013-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In 2013, Gary Carr (the creative director of <a href="/wiki/Lionhead_Studios" title="Lionhead Studios">Lionhead Studios</a>) predicted that within the next 5 to 10 years, the games development workforce would be 50% female.<sup id="cite_ref-variety2013_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-variety2013-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Gamasutra" title="Gamasutra">Gamasutra</a>'s Game Developer Salary Survey 2014, women in the United States made 86 cents for every dollar men made. Game designing women had the closest equity, making 96 cents for every dollar men made in the same job, while audio professional women had the largest gap, making 68% of what men in the same position made.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
